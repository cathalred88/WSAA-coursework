Cathal Kelleher had never thought of himself as remarkable. He was thirty-two, lived in a narrow terraced house at the edge of town, and worked in a small repair shop where he fixed radios that no one else had the patience to understand. His life moved at the steady rhythm of ticking clocks and soldering irons. If you’d asked anyone to describe him, they might have said, “Quiet,” or perhaps, “Kind, in a distracted way.”

The bicycle arrived on a Tuesday.

Cathal found it leaning against the brick wall behind his shop when he stepped out to empty the bin. It was the kind of bicycle you might see in an old photograph—curved handlebars, a slender steel frame painted a deep emerald green, and a leather saddle worn smooth with time. A small brass plaque gleamed just beneath the handlebars. It read:

*For Cathal. Ride wisely.*

He blinked. He was quite certain he hadn’t ordered a bicycle.

He wheeled it inside, meaning only to keep it safe until someone claimed it. Yet the moment his fingers closed around the grips, a peculiar warmth traveled up his arms, as if the bicycle were greeting him. He laughed nervously at the thought.

That evening, after closing the shop, Cathal decided to ride it home. The tires rolled as softly as whispers. The chain made no sound at all. It felt less like riding and more like gliding through air that parted politely to let him pass.

Halfway down Main Street, a man in a grey suit stumbled from a doorway, shouting into his phone.

“You incompetent fool!” the man roared. “You’ve ruined everything!”

The words seemed to crackle in the air, sharp and bitter. Cathal winced as he pedaled past. And then, just as the anger reached its loudest pitch, something extraordinary happened.

From the spokes of the emerald bicycle, a faint golden shimmer spun outward. The angry words twisted midair like startled birds. There was a soft *pop*—not unlike a cork leaving a bottle—and where the shout had been, a neatly wrapped ham sandwich tumbled gently onto the pavement.

Cathal braked so abruptly he nearly fell.

The man in the grey suit froze mid-rant. His phone slipped from his hand.

“Did you see—?” the man began.

But there was nothing left of the anger. Only a sandwich, resting innocently beside the curb.

Cathal dismounted slowly. He crouched beside the sandwich. It was perfect: thick slices of ham, buttered bread, a crisp leaf of lettuce. He glanced up at the bicycle.

“That wasn’t normal,” he said.

The bicycle, being a bicycle, did not reply.

The man in the grey suit stared at Cathal, then at the sandwich, then at Cathal again. “I… I suddenly don’t feel so cross,” he muttered, rubbing his temple. “In fact, I’m rather hungry.”

Cathal handed him the sandwich.

The man blinked, then gave a sheepish smile. “Thank you.” And just like that, he walked away, chewing thoughtfully instead of shouting.

Cathal looked down at the emerald frame. “You turn hate into ham sandwiches,” he whispered.

The bicycle gleamed in the fading sunlight, as if pleased to have been understood.

—

The next morning, Cathal tested his theory.

He rode to the bus station at rush hour, where tempers flared as reliably as the timetable failed. A woman scolded a driver. Two teenagers argued about whose fault it was they’d missed their bus. A man swore at a parking meter that refused his coins.

Cathal pedaled gently through the noise.

Golden light flickered from the spinning wheels. Each angry word softened, puffed, and dropped to the pavement as a freshly made ham sandwich. Within minutes, the station platform was dotted with neatly wrapped parcels.

The remarkable thing was not only the sandwiches—it was the silence that followed. Without their anger, people seemed unsure what to do. They blinked, looked around, and then, sheepishly, they laughed. A few picked up the sandwiches and shared them. Someone apologized. The teenagers shrugged and began discussing what film to see instead.

Cathal felt a strange mixture of triumph and responsibility. This was no ordinary gift. It was a remedy.

Word spread quickly.

By the end of the week, people had noticed that whenever Cathal cycled through town, arguments dissolved. The bakery owner swore she’d seen a sandwich fall from a heated dispute about sourdough. A teacher claimed her classroom quarrel vanished in a puff of flour-dusted bread.

Soon, whenever tempers rose, someone would say, “Fetch Cathal.”

He did not mind. In fact, he felt more alive than ever. Each ride became a quiet mission. He cycled past construction sites where foremen barked orders. He pedaled near council meetings thick with accusation. He even rode, trembling slightly, past a protest where chants burned hot and fierce.

Each time, the bicycle shimmered. Each time, anger transformed. The streets filled with sandwiches, and with them, something softer—laughter, surprise, a shared meal.

But the magic had limits.

One evening, Cathal rode home exhausted. The day had been filled with shouting, too much shouting. As he turned onto his street, he heard raised voices from his own house.

His brother Michael stood on the doorstep, face flushed.

“You never listen!” Michael shouted as Cathal approached. “You disappear all day playing hero on that ridiculous bike, and you don’t think about anyone else!”

The words struck harder than any he’d heard that week.

Cathal instinctively pedaled forward.

The bicycle shimmered faintly—but no sandwich appeared.

Michael’s anger hung heavy in the air.

Cathal stopped. “Why didn’t it work?” he whispered to the bicycle.

And then he understood.

The magic did not exist to erase conflict. It existed to transform hatred freely given. But this—this was hurt. This was something that needed listening, not alchemy.

Cathal leaned the bicycle gently against the wall.

“I’m sorry,” he said to Michael. “I didn’t realize you felt that way.”

The silence that followed was uncomfortable, but real. They talked for a long time—about responsibility, about feeling overlooked, about the strange weight of being needed by everyone.

No golden light shimmered. No sandwich fell.

Yet something changed all the same.

—

After that night, Cathal rode differently.

He still cycled through town, still let the bicycle soften the sharpest edges of fury. But he no longer chased every raised voice. Sometimes people needed to speak their anger. Sometimes they needed to hear it echo before they could let it go.

The town became known, in time, for its curious calm. Visitors would remark on how disagreements seemed to end in shared lunches. Children would search the pavements after arguments, hoping to find a neatly wrapped treasure.

And Cathal?

He remained a quiet man in a narrow house. He still repaired radios. He still preferred listening to speaking.

But when hatred flared too bright, when it threatened to scorch rather than warm, he would wheel out the emerald bicycle and ride.

The brass plaque beneath the handlebars caught the light as he passed.

*Ride wisely.*

And wherever anger rose like smoke, the wheels would spin gold, and from the air itself would fall something simple and sustaining—bread, ham, and the possibility of a gentler world.
